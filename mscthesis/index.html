BACKGROUND • LPP and TTP
LPP LINE PLANNING PROBLEM
  • Bespoke knapsack problem: find the optimal configuration of capacity and frequency for
    minimising cost while maximising service to passengers according to demand between stations.
    • disregarding feasibility (operational constraints)
    • M. R. Bussieck et al. “Optimal Lines for Railway Systems”, 1997
      European Journal of Operational Research 96, 54-63
      • two models: cost minimisation or direct connections maximisation
    • J.-W. Goossens et al. “A Branch-and-Cut Approach for Solving Line Planning Problems.”, 2004
      Transportation Science 38, 379-393
      • cost minimisation with constrained demand, one train type, predetermined rides
  • Answers the questions
    What lines to operate? How frequently? With what capacity?
    to serve all passengers with the minimum cost.
   × 
BACKGROUND • LPP and TTP
TTP TRAIN TIMETABLING PROBLEM
  • Combinatorial problem to determine departures and arrivals of a predetermined set of trains,
    usually on a single line, with varying objectives
    • e.g. minimising travel time, cost, energy, sometimes tackled as multi-objective
    • M. Carey, D. Lockwood “A Model, Algorithms and Strategy for Train Pathing”, 1995
      Journal of the Operational Research Society 46, 988-1005
      • first big-m formulation
    • U. Brännlund et al. “Railway Timetabling Using Lagrangian Relaxation.”, 1998
      Transportation Science 32, 358-369
      • first time-indexed formulation, block sections and time intervals
  • Answers the question
    What time should trains arrive and depart to and from stations?
    to minimise passenger travel time, given physical and logistical constraints.

BACKGROUND • LPP and TTP
LPP + TTP INTEGRATED, INDUSTRY ORIENTED
  • Iterative or truly integrated solutions to find valid optimal line configurations,
    generating the lines and the corresponding (usually periodic) timetables
    • LPP is inherently periodic, TTP ensures feasibility
    • M. Kaspi, T. Raviv “Service-Oriented Line Planning and Timetabling for Passenger Trains”, 2013
      Transportation Science 47, 295-311
      • first integrated method with heuristics
    • F. Yan, R. M. P. Goverde “Combined line planning and train timetabling
      for strongly heterogeneous railway lines with direct connections.”, 2019
      Transportation Research Part B: Methodological 127, 20-46
      • multi-frequency line planning and multi-period timetabling, least capacity loss
    • Z. Huang, H. Zheng “Integrated Line Planning and Timetable Scheduling for Railways
      Considering the Dynamics and Uncertainty of Passenger Demand”, 2025
      IET Intelligent Trans Sys. 19, e70019
      • focus on robustness via DL-based passenger demand estimation

BACKGROUND • RL
REINFORCEMENT LEARNING FORMULATION
  • Environment encompasses the infrastructure
    • representing topology and dynamics
  • Single agent acts as a dispatcher
    • viewing the network and controlling train movements
  • States represent occupancy and relevant information
    • about the system or a portion of it
  • Rewards are generated according to episode outcome
    • penalties for early termination and suboptimal conditions

BACKGROUND • RL
REINFORCEMENT LEARNING PITFALLS
  • States grow with network size
    • noisy and hard to maintain with big and complex infrastructure
  • Credit assignment becomes unreliable
    • single reward for a complex scenario
  • Policy could lack expressivity
    • poor generalisation across the scenario
  • Actions are imprecise
    • when referred a system made up of multiple different entities

BACKGROUND • MARL
MULTI-AGENT REINFORCEMENT LEARNING (DIS)ADVANTAGES
    UPSIDES
  • Decision-making decentralisation
    • reducing state and action spaces
  • Generalisable policies
    • with parameter sharing
  • Realistic formulation
    • reflecting dispatch operations
  • Modularity and expandability
    • changes to single repeated unit
    DOWNSIDES
  • Coordination might be hard to establish
    • with limited perception and increasing agents
  • Train-centric approaches are less realistic
    • learning independent movement
  • Train-centric approaches have poor scalability
    • as trains to be scheduled change

    • encompassing the entire network
  • Actions and policy apply to the system
    • agent acts as a line coordinator
